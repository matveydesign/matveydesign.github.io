Education Perfect

Homeschooling Web App

Research into product-market fit for EP’s expansion into the Homeschooler area.
Education Perfect has a strong focus on their suite of tools for schools, teachers, and students. But what about parents and guardians? They’re a key part in students’ success in education and overall social health, yet had not received as much attention.

A common problem we heard from teachers was the lack of parent/guardian involvement in their children’s education process. This was not solely down to their motivation, but also a number of other factors: school system set-up, a lack of education system transparency, teacher & school management politics, and time poorness being among these. It posed a greater challenge than we initially anticipated.

The project was a year-long research and ideation process responding to the following problem statements:
How do we involve parents/guardians into the education process?
How do we make them feel engaged and valued?
Simply put, how do we excite them?

Have a look at our journey trying to solve this challenge below.

Desired Outcome

A Homeschooler and school-student extension web app, for parents and students.

Case study

The project started off with a team of two: myself, a Product Designer, and a Product Manager. The Leadership posed the challenge of ‘adapting our existing tools for the Homeschooler or School-student extension (extra-curricular education) market’. It became clear quickly that the challenge lay somewhere else entirely – parent/guardian disengagement with the education system.After the initial round of interviews with school leaders, school parents, homeschooling parents, and their kids we started to narrow down the challenges. We also became familiar with various family make-ups: two parents, single parents, grandparent caretakers, legal guardians etc. (Note: this case study will refer to all these groups as ‘parents’, however we were mindful to use the correct language to refer to particular user groups within the product itself.)

Main challenges

  1. Parents, even the most engaged ones, have no indication of how their kids are doing in school beyond the basic term reports.
  2. Kids don’t share their school progress with parents. It’s often a dead-end conversation.
  3. Teachers and schools are reluctant to extend their communication to parents beyond existing practices, mainly due to time poorness, but also due to each school’s management politics.

For me it became clear that the problem to solve was the breakdown in communication between 3 key participators in the student education process: teachers, students, and parents.

Due to the open-ended nature of the challenge, we chose to undertake blue-sky ideation. Business requirements were pushing us to adapt the existing suite of tools for the Homeschooler market, which we extrapolated to a key opportunity: more granular parent reporting on student progress. This led to a new team being stood up to drive this initiative, which we directed.

Synchronising teacher-student-parent progress communication across product suites proved to be a technical challenge which our code-base wasn’t set up to support. We decided to pivot to a standalone parent experience and chose to focus on a mobile platform. This was because mobile-available services are uniquely positioned to cut through challenges with lack of access or time, and our research showed an already high usage of mobile devices in the parent cohort. This also unblocked a set of technical challenges, as our team already had prior experience developing the iOS / Android Teacher app.

We conducted 3 rounds of remote user-testing using our mobile prototypes. I set up a remote testing environment on users’ personal mobile devices through Lookback and Zoom, and ensured the prototype behaviour matched the native app experience as closely as possible so we could elicit accurate feedback.

Summary of testing outcomes:

  1. Parents loved having added visibility of their children’s school progress and performance.
  2. The mobile app format was a success.
  3. However – there was simply not enough relevant content coming from our platform to keep parents engaged long-term. And certainly not enough to warrant a new product.

With these findings alone we convinced the leadership team to pivot and give us more time to figure out the uncovered challenge: lack of engaging parent content.

Based on our extensive research in the space and updated business requirements we chose to explore the idea of a responsive web app. We had to drop the mobile-only approach due to the increased concept difficulty.

This time the concept targeted not just parents, but also students.

We decided to focus on student and parent engagement features and using the right motivational language for both cohorts.

We ran 3 more rounds of remote concept validation and usability tests using 2 interactive prototypes of student and parent experiences. Engaging around 25 participants, we tested:

  1. Self-directed courses
  2. Content revision
  3. Student wellbeing
  4. Extensive parent reporting
  5. Gamification opportunities

Parents were excited about the self-directed courses because it minimised the dependence on their active involvement, and the kids (especially boys) responded with enthusiasm to the gamification aspects.

We received incredibly positive user feedback on the web app’s visual presentation – it was clean and functional, yet not devoid of personality which was key for younger students. Our team took that as a strong indication we were on the right track.

Ultimately we came close to finalising our concepts and presenting these to leadership. Ongoing ad-hoc user testing increased our confidence in the success of the product.

Unfortunately, a number of factors outside our team’s control resulted in the project stagnating and reaching an indefinite pause.

Retro

In my eyes, the project was a success – we uncovered the right challenges, completed rounds of rigorous research and solved every question posed to our team.

I’ve honed my user testing skills and became confident in running fully remote medium-scale usability studies. The best opportunities were presented to us when interviewing homeschooling parents – their kids in the room with them, studying, playing; parents casually included them in the interviews process, which perfectly fit our blue-sky ideation stages.

The gamification features gave me an opportunity to explore in-depth game theory. Key insight became a point of intense discussion – gamification. As most commonly understand it, it’s not simply achievements and progress, rather something more encompassing. It’s a system and a collection of processes that support the larger goal of an experience/tool/product/game, hence it would be a mistake at worst, and tech debt at least, to include a gamification layer atop a system not supporting it from the ground up.

I genuinely hope to have more opportunities to conduct in-depth user research.
